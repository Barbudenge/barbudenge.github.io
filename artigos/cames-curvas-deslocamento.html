Displacement curves in cams

The displacement curve is the specification of the movement that the cam should impose. It defines not only position, but also speed, acceleration and smoothness of the cycle.

By Artur Henrique de Freitas Avelar Updated May 2026 Category: cams

In cam synthesis, the physical contour is constructed from a movement curve. This curve informs how much the follower moves for each angle of the cam, and its derivatives inform the speed, acceleration and associated jerks.

Displacement, speed and acceleration

If \(S(\theta)\) is the displacement of the follower, the speed relative to the rotation of the cam depends on \(dS/d\theta\), and the acceleration depends on \(d^2S/d\theta^2\). In a real machine, these derivatives matter because inertial forces, vibration, and contact are directly affected by the shape of the curve.

\[ S(\theta) \quad \rightarrow \quad \frac{dS}{d\theta} \quad \rightarrow \quad \frac{d^2S}{d\theta^2} \]

A curve that appears acceptable on the displacement graph may produce sudden changes in speed or acceleration. That's why the design methods check the combination of displacement, speed, acceleration and, in more demanding applications, jerk.

Dwell and constant velocity

Dwell intervals have constant displacement, zero speed and zero acceleration. They are useful when the machine needs to wait for an operation: filling, pressing, cutting, inspection or transfer.

Constant speed sections seem simple, but they cannot be connected directly to dwells without transition. Going from zero speed to constant speed would instantly imply infinite acceleration in the ideal model. In practice, it is necessary to use transition curves before and after them.

Cycloidal motion

Cycloidal movement is widely used because it can start and end with zero speed and acceleration over a complete stretch. This facilitates connecting with dwells and reduces severe discontinuities. It is especially useful in cycles with rise, dwell, return, and dwell again.

When the design requires a smooth transition between dwell and motion, full or partial cycloidal curves are often good candidates. The choice depends on the previous and subsequent section.

Harmonic movement

Simple harmonic motion also appears frequently in introductory courses. It has a clear geometric interpretation and allows you to construct rise or return sections with compact equations. In some connections, however, the acceleration at the beginning or end may not match the neighboring section.

Therefore, it is not enough to choose "harmonic" because the curve is known. It is necessary to check whether the speed and acceleration conditions at the connection points are compatible with the remainder of the cycle.

Eighth-degree polynomial

Higher-order polynomial curves allow you to control more boundary conditions. The eighth degree polynomial appears as an alternative when you want to adjust displacement, speed and acceleration at specific points, including with asymmetric behavior.

The advantage is flexibility. The disadvantage is that the result may lose geometric intuition and needs to be checked carefully, especially on acceleration and curvature.

An acceptable displacement curve is one that connects well to what comes before and after. The isolated section can be beautiful and still be unsuitable for the complete cycle.

Conceptual example: double dwell

Consider a filling mechanism in which a can must wait, advance to the filling position, remain stationary during the process, and return. This cycle naturally calls for two dwells and two motion intervals. A pure linear trial generates abrupt transitions. A better solution uses gentle curves to move forward and return, interspersed with dwell intervals.

This example shows the function of synthesis: transforming an operational sequence into a \(S(\theta)\) curve that respects displacement, time, smoothness and geometric feasibility.

Practical selection criteria

  • use dwell when the machine needs to keep follower stopped;
  • avoid connecting a constant-velocity segment directly to a dwell;
  • prefer curves with compatible speed and acceleration at the junction points;
  • check whether speed and acceleration peaks are acceptable;
  • then evaluate the pressure angle and the resulting profile curvature.

Page summary

  • The displacement curve defines position, velocity and acceleration.
  • Dwell and constant velocity require compatible transitions.
  • Cycloidal, harmonic and polynomial meet different conditions.
